What Is Debate and Why It Matters

Debate is a structured way to compare ideas and make decisions. It builds critical thinking, empathy, collaboration, and quick, confident speaking. In this lesson, you will use a simple argument structure and practice turning ideas into short, persuasive speeches.

The ARESW Framework

Use ARESW to construct every argument:
- Assertion: Your claim or main idea (what you want the audience to accept).
- Reason: Why the claim should be true (the logic that links to the claim).
- Evidence: Proof supporting the reason (facts, data, examples, expert sources).
- Significance: Why it matters (who is affected, how big the effect is, and why we should care).
- Weighing: Compare your outcome against the other sideâ€™s outcomes to show yours is better or more important.
Example (motion: THW ban smoking in Hong Kong):
- Assertion: Banning smoking will save the city money.
- Reason: Smoking causes health problems that increase demand on the public health system.
- Evidence: Health authorities report that for every death caused by smoking, many more live with serious smoking-related illnesses (cancer, heart disease, COPD, etc.).
- Significance: Fewer patients means lower public costs and faster access to care for those in need.
- Weighing: Savings can be redirected to scholarships and expanded services, benefiting more people over a longer time than the short-term inconvenience to smokers.

How to Weigh Competing Arguments

Weighing explains why your outcome is superior. Compare using:
- Magnitude: How big is the benefit or harm?
- Probability: How likely is the outcome?
- Timeframe: How soon will it happen and for how long?
- Reversibility: Can the harm be undone if we are wrong?
- Stakeholders: Who is affected most (children, vulnerable groups, taxpayers, patients, etc.)?
Tip: Use clear phrasesâ€”"Even if their benefit exists, ours helps more people sooner," or "Our harm is less likely and reversible; theirs is widespread and long-lasting."

Build an Argument: Step-by-Step Template

Use this sentence frame to draft a 60â€“90 second point:
1) Assertion: I support/oppose the motion because [claim].
2) Reason: This is because [reason explaining the mechanism].
3) Evidence: For example/According to [source or example], [evidence].
4) Significance: As a result, [who] experience(s) [impact], which matters because [why it changes decisions or outcomes].
5) Weighing: Compared to the other side, our outcome is better because it is [bigger/more likely/sooner/affects more vulnerable people/less risky].

Practice Motions and Idea Starters

Try these motions using ARESW:
- THW ban alcohol: Consider public health costs vs. individual liberty and black markets.
- This House, as schools, will ban all contact sports (e.g., boxing, rugby): Consider student safety, skill development, culture, and alternatives.
Beginner scaffold: Start with one strong ARESW argument on one side.
Intermediate extension: Build two ARESW arguments and prepare one refutation using weighing.

ðŸ§  Knowledge Check

Which sequence correctly describes ARES?

ARES stands for Assertion (claim), Reason (logic), Evidence (proof), Significance (impact).

Which of the following is the best example of Evidence?

Evidence is verifiable support such as data, studies, or expert testimony.

What is the purpose of Weighing in a debate?

Weighing compares impacts across size, likelihood, timeframe, and stakeholders to justify prioritization.

In the argument "Ban smoking will save money because fewer people will need treatment," which part is Significance?

Significance explains why the claim matters: fewer patients means cost savings and improved access.

You say: "Schools should ban contact sports because they cause concussions. A study shows 1 in 5 athletes reported symptoms last season." What is missing to complete ARESW?

You have assertion, reason, and evidence. Add significance (why this matters) and weighing (why it outweighs the benefits).
